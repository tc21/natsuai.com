Having three sets and a pair, and two spare tiles is called a “kuttsuki”[1] shape. From such a shape, you enter tenpai by drawing a tile next to one of those spare tiles, or by drawing your pair into a triplet. Generally, you'll be choosing between two or three tiles to drop every turn, and you want to make sure to play the most efficient tile in terms of tile efficiency and final tenpai shape.

Let's look at this table. We can't touch the manzu shape, which contains a dora, so we're choosing between n and h to discard. One turn ago, we chose h to discard when there wasn't much difference between the two. This turn, we've drawn h again. Some people would instinctively discard h, but is that really the right choice?

If you look closely at the table, you'll notice that the turn that just passed has created a big difference between the two tiles.

Let's look at pinzu. We now see three m discarded, which greatly reduces the value of n. , is basically useless. b isn't that great of a draw either. m is still a good draw, but it's unlikely to happen with only one left. What about souzu? Plenty of g and j remain, and with three h visible from our point-of-view, j and k become difficult for our opponents to use. This means that in addition to ryanmen waits, a kanchan wait after drawing k is also worth waiting for.

Therefore, you should keep h and discard n here. The only drawback of keeping h is if you draw another h, you become furiten. But that is quite unlikely to happen, since there's only one h, compared to gjk, so it's not something to worry about.

On the other hand, drawing f is a bit less straightforward. If it looks like you'll still have time to build your hand, then don't take the tenpai and wait for a better draw with the manzu shape. Otherwise, riichi on the kanchan g wait.
