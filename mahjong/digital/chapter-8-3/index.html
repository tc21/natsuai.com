However, you drew e resulting in B. You might say “Oh no!” and toss e without thinking. However, if the dealer has called twice, including a dora pon, your opponents are probably starting to defend. It's unlikely that they'll let you call a b. Is there anything you can do?

You should discard n. There isn't a big difference in tile efficiency, so you should take advantage of the w being easier to call now that you've already discarded it. If you draw sg first, you can just wait on a q and v shanpon, so there's no need to worry about furiten.

Soon after, you draw b. What now? w, which you've already discarded, vs. n, which you've already discarded. They're equally easy to call, so it's better to allow the possibility of getting c, and discard e this time. Again, if you draw sg first, you can choose between a shanpon wait or a furiten ryanmen wait.

By the way, I want to talk about how Takamizawa-pro played in the December 2013 Shou'ou Ketteisen[1]. He was first in the total ranking, and was being heavily marked by the other players. His hand was C.

For some reason, his discards was qrx5. And he discarded yu! Since he couldn't hope to chii or ron ti, he chose to keep the two shapes that are easy to call. I, his kamicha, broke up my hand to bail, discarding his genbutsu r. He was waiting to chii, and then discarded the second 5! Surely he drew the 5 after discarding yu, but we were all very confused. With the other three players desperately bailing, Takamizawa was able to freely play his hand until ryuukyoku.

Even though I'm able to recognize that he probably made the right play, I'm not sure I would be able to do the same thing myself. Although I had no idea what was going on when it happened, I'm impressed by what he did now that I've had a second look.
