If you have previously discarded a tile that would your hand, you cannot call ron. It was with the invention of this rule in the late 1940s that (riichi) mahjong gained the complexity of attacking vs. defending, and started to become the interesting game we know today.

However, many people have suffered under the furiten[1] rule. My most recent chonbo was a furiten ron[2]. I was defending against a riichi, and made an unexpected tenpai, and immediately someone dealt in. I said “ron!” but it turns out that's a tile I've discarded already...

Look at this hand please. You've already discarded z. What should you discard now? Should you discard xc to avoid furiten, or should you discard qw to keep ryanmen?

First let's consider the situation where you draw hl first. If you kept qw, you can declare a penchan-wait riichi. But I don't think it's a much more attractive option than a furiten zv riichi. Additionally, if you don't declare riichi, zv is better than e since you won't be able to win unless you tsumo anyway. In other words, if you draw hl first, there's not too much difference between keeping xc and qw.

On the other hand, let's think about making this hand into a good hand that waits on hl. If you keep qw, unless you draw one of the four remaining e, you won't be waiting on hl. Keeping xc allows you to draw any of seven remaining zv. Thus it's better to keep the xc, which is easy to take care of.

  1. Drawing hl and declaring riichi → Little difference between penchan e and zv ryanmen.
  2. Drawing hl and staying datamen → zv ryanmen is better.
  3. Getting to hl tenpai first → keeping zv ryanmen is better.
Comparing the two options

Thus when given the hand at the beginning, you should discard qw. Whether you consider the likelihood of getting rid of the bad shape by drawing into it, or the strength of your tenpai wait on average, it's better to keep the ryanmen shape. To be more specific, you should discard q. If you draw e the next turn, you should look at the table to compare qr against zv, and keep the better ryanmen.

  1. [^] Kobayashi explains here, that “furu” means “throw away”, and “furiten” means “tenpai when you've thrown away your winning tile”, and that the concept of furiten doesn't exist unless you are tenpai, so you shouldn't call something furiten unless they're already tenpai.
