Urasuji (裏スジ, lit. “inside suji”) is a concept with a long history, but it turns out few people properly understand it. For example, if you have sfg, you will discard s to wait on dh. This kind of ryanmen wait adjacent to a discarded tile is called urasuji. In the previous example, the urasuji of s is dh. In this section, I'd like to talk about how to use the concept of urasuji.

In the early turns, we discard single, unconnected tiles, starting from the least useful. In general, most people discard in the order of honor tiles → 1 and 9 → 2 and 8 → 3 through 7. The following is a discard pile of someone who declared riichi. How should you think about this riichi? All tiles, other than 1 and q are tedashi.

4ab5wi1qH

The first tile that catches the eye is the riichi declaration tile h. But there is a tile that I want you to pay more attention to in this discard pile. That is the third-turn discard b. Why would they discard this middle-of-the-pack number tile this early? If b was just an ordinary single tile, 5 and w should have been discarded before it. Even if they had a bbn shape, discarding b there for the ryanmen shape is rare. Usually you would hold on to it. So they have a shape that doesn't lose much tile efficiency by discarding b. In other words, they likely had xcb or bm,. With regards to this riichi, the urasuji of b, that is zv and n., are particularly dangerous. Like this, if someone discards an easy-to-use 3 through 7 tile early, its urasuji becomes dangerous. On the other hand, waits that happen across that discard become less dangerous. Think about this discard pile:

4l5wi1qB

Unlike the first example, this time the b looks to be a tile that could have been useful right up until the riichi. Thus it's unlikely to have been discarded from a xcb or bm, shape. If (for example) they're waiting on zv, it would likely be because they improved a cb kanchan shape by drawing x, but those cases are uncommon. Today, we value first riichi, and most people would immediately declare riichi, even with a kanchan shape. Thus, when paying attention to a b riichi declaration tile, you should be careful about its matagi-suji (跨ぎスジ, lit. “across-suji”). That is, since shapes like vbb and bbn are common, cn and vm are dangerous.

(Translator's note: urasuji and matagi-suji have been part of the most popular yet controversial theories/pseudotheories in mahjong. They are difficult to understand, and easy to misuse. Kobayashi talks about this in the next section, so please read on if you've read this section. Also, if you want a better understanding of these concepts, mahjonggem.com (Jemma Sakamoto) has articles on urasuji, matagi-suji, and why you shouldn't trust urasuji.)
