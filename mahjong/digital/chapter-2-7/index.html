Like ippatsu, haitei is something we'd like to break up if we can. If you are approaching the end of a hand and you're not tenpai, then there's no drawback to ruining up your hand by breaking someone else's haitei. So haitei is something we definitely want to break.

You will need to recognize who has the haitei draw. Now, do you know who gets the haitei draw if a hand goes to ryuukyoku[1], without any calls from anyone?

It's the player sitting at south. with 14 tiles in the dead wall, that means the south-player's 18th draw is the haitei tile. A chi pushes the haitei to the next person, a pon from the player across sends haitei to the player across, and a pon from a previous player moves the haitei tile one seat backwards. After you figure out who has the haitei draw, you especially want to prevent it from being drawn by a player in riichi.

Please look at this situation. The dealer declared riichi on turn 6. They called k into a kan on turn 8. After that, everyone else bailed their hands. Unfortunately the haitei tile will be the dealer's next draw. You weren't able to break haitei, so it there anything you can do? You have a lot of safe tiles, but which one is shimocha[2] most likely to call? They don't look like they have any souzu, so you probably want to discard b or n. There are three n visible from your viewpoint, so it's less likely that they have it. n is the most likely to be called.

It's very tempting to say n is the correct discard here, but think more carefully. Even if shimocha calls n here, the dealer still has one more draw. Is there a way to prevent even that from happening? The key here is the 1-triplet. Consider that the dealer's riichi declaration tile is 3. The dealer, in east round, would not riichi with a tanki wait on the shonpai 1 when they already see a 3discarded.[3] They would wait on the 3.[4] Therefore, 1 is basically a safe tile. Discard 1, and you can pon it if anyone else also discards it! Then you will have prevented the dealer's next draw entirely from happening. As long as the person declaring riichi is one that plays reliably, this is a very useful technique. Learn to use it when you play.
