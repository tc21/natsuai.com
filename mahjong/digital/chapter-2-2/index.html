Compared to honba points, deposited riichi sticks[1] have a bigger effect. When there are two or three riichi sticks unclaimed on the table, you will have to think about points differently.

Consider a situation with 1 honba and 2 riichi sticks. If you win with only pinfu, your hand isn't just 1000 points, but 1000+300+2000. In other words, a 3300-point damaten hand. When thinking about whether to riichi or not, this is no time to be afraid of attacking with just pinfu. Instead, your should view your hand as a 4300-point hand and push.

Also, a key point here is that unlike honba points, deposited riichi sticks don't increase the points you lose when you deal in. So, the more riichi sticks on the table, the bigger your hand becomes, without having to worry about dealing in to bigger hands. This makes it more worthwhile to push your hand.

Please take a look at this situation. You have called 5 and are 1-shanten for toitoi. Usually there's no reason not to discard z: 5-nomi is only 1300 points, but adding toitoi makes it 5200 points. Even if you miss out on the kanchan-x draw, the four-times-as-large hand is more than worth it. When you get to tenpai, you would discard u and have a shanpon wait.

But here we are in 2 honba, and have 2 riichi sticks. Winning anything gives you 2000+600 more points. 5-nomi is already 3900 points! If adding the two han from toitoi only about doubles the value of your hand, then there's no point in obsessing over it. We should discard y and decide on the ryanmen shape. Instead of drawing or calling the single y that remains, we would rather not miss out on the three remaining x that we could draw or chii for a ti tenpai. We should be trying to win as fast as possible, too, to deny our opponents from taking the 2000 points on the table.

  1. [^] “Deposited” riichi sticks refer to riichi sticks put onto the table that have remained on the table for various reasons, such as ryuukyoku.
