With the exception of chiitoitsu and kokushi-musou, all mahjong hands have a shape of “4 sets and 1 pair”. This “4 sets and 1 pair” combined makes 5 blocks. It is very important to think of mahjong as making 5 blocks.

Many people might look at the hand above and want to play g to secure a two-sided shape, but my recommendation here is to discard qw. At a glance it might seem like a loss in tile efficiency, it's important to consider the progression of the hand later on. Let's consider what would happen if you discarded g, and then drew a ryanmen shape, or the 7 set.

Either way, it looks like you will end up discarding qw. Even if instead of g you discarded m the situation would be the same. The only situation where you won't discard qw would be if you drew e.

We can also look at it from another perspective. After discarding g, you would be tsumogiri-ing[1] until you complete your next set. Tiles that can form a pair like yuxc, or safe tiles like 3, would all have to be discarded. If we had discarded qw instead, we would lose the e draw, but gain a “free spot” in our hand for any tile we want. This is especially helpful if we wanted to keep a safe tile, or the dora 6. In short, discarding 5s makes your hand appear more efficient, but actually constrains your hand, making it too inflexible. Discarding qw is less efficient for now, but gives you the flexibility to handle whatever tile that happens to come your way.

Why is such a thing happening? If you look at the hand at the very beginning, you'll see that the hand has 6 sets of tiles that could potentially become a complete set (i.e. 6 “blocks”): qw, yu, xc, m.., fgg, 77. It is impossible to utilize all 6 blocks in your hand. You must give up one one of them in order to win. In most cases, you will end up giving up the qw block. In such a situation, you should give up on the weaker block that you would most likely end up giving up anyway, and hold on to the 5 stronger blocks in your hand.
