Tedashi vs. tsumogiri is an important hint towards correctly reading your opponents' hands. If you memorize the tedashi and tsumogiri status of every tile from every opponent, and react accordingly, certainly your results will be better. But, to be honest, memorizing every tile is just too much work.

For those of you who think that way, start by trying to just remember the tsumogiri and tedashi of opponents you decide to pay attention to. The opponents you should be paying attention to include those in direct competition with you for placement, those that have called twice, and those that have called with a possibly large hand. It's a good idea to start by practicing memorizing discards by following this rule.

After calling the double-1, we are 1-shanten with dora 2. We can also call into tenpai. We draw f. We should be discarding v or f. It looks like neither souzu nor pinzu are better than the other, so there's not much difference in how likely we are to draw cn compared to dh. We only need to think about how much information our discards give away.

First, let's think about discarding v. The pinzu tedashi will make your opponents cautious of its surrounding tiles, and discarding f when your hand progresses will make them cautious of souzu as well. Tedashi of f v will cause the same effect, making their surrounding tiles difficult to come out, and unlikely for you to chii or ron.

With the above considerations, the best thing to do here is f tsumogiri. Either way people will be cautious about tiles next to your tenpai discard v, but you don't give away any information regarding souzu, so dh are much easier to come out compared to the other options of playing your hand.

Even when discarding the same tile, tedashi vs. tsumogiri affects how much information you give away about your hand. You cannot prevent your opponents being on guard against your tenpai discard. Even so, try to give away as little information about your hand as possible, so you gain an advantage if you draw into a dh tenpai. Ideally, you should tsumogiri without any noticeable pause.
