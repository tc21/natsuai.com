Let's look at hand A. You have enough blocks, but not any completed sets. This is 3-shanten for a normal hand. You have four pairs, so it's 2-shanten for chiitoitsu. Although you want to enable both chiitoitsu and a standard hand, you have so many ryanmen shapes, as well as a yakuhai pair. Breaking up a ryanmen for chiitoitsu would be too wasteful. We will have to choose to use our ryanmen shapes, and give up on chiitoitsu. You can discard q, ,, or g. Which one should you discard?

Discarding , results in hand B. This looks like a good shape, but it already has six blocks. If you want to win, you have to break up one of the blocks. Most likely you will have to discard qq from this hand. Might as well have discarded qq to begin with, holding on to the five strongest blocks.

In other words, discarding , or g to give up on chiitoitsu probably means you will break up the q pair as well, making chiitoitsu basically impossible. On the other hand, breaking up the q pair means you have space for an extra tile in your hand, and you could possibly draw a pair again, causing you to rethink your plan of going with a standard hand. All of a sudden, we are 1-shanten for chiitoitsu which we once decided to give up on. This hand is still 3-shanten for a normal hand, we should probably switch to prioritizing chiitoitsu here, and think about which unpaired tile to discard. This way, we discarded a pair from hand A, but still kept the possibility of chiitoitsu alive. Discarding , or g would never be able to achieve this. Please remember this shape.
