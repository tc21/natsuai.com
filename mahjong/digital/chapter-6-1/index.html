“Camouflage” is a term that's not used as much as it used to be. On a battlefield, camouflage conceals your own movements, making it difficult for your enemies to notice. In the 1980s, camouflages, or techniques to prevent opponents from reading your hands, were highly rated, and often used.

qeruuoxccmsl34

Look at this hand. The tactics of camouflaging is to discard cru from this hand, and go for chanta or a 123 sanshoku. But nowadays we prioritize speed, and this playstyle became detrimental. To begin with, making a 123 sanshoku from this hand is extremely difficult, not to mention that if you end up waiting on souzu, your hard-worked camouflage would be for naught. Rather than slowing down your hand, it's better to make use of the er ryanmen, and treat xcc as two potential sets, and discard 34 or l. Such is the mainstream.

That said, it would be premature to say that camouflage is dead, and a thing of the past. In my opinion, there are many useful camouflaging techniques that still work today, in the age of racing to be the first tenpai and first riichi.

In relation to camouflaging, there is a discard technique called “sakigiri”[1]. This mainly refers to confirming a ryanmen shape before you get to tenpai, for example, discarding c from xcc. That alone isn't enough conceal your wait for sure, but when the sakigiri happens very early on, it can be very effective[2].

For example, if this was your discard pile (every tile is tedashi):

3cl7ift

People will be cautious of the tiles around f and t, tiles you needed until you were tenpai (or very close), but people are unlikely to think you are waiting on zv or xb, when you discarded c that early. This kind of sakigiri is also a type of camouflage, and it's quite effective even today. However, if you overuse it, you'll end up losing tile efficiency by discarding a c that you would have been able to use. You have to at least prioritize getting to four sets and a pair.
